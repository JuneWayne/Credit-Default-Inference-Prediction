**Inference vs Prediction**

This project shows that inference and prediction are related, but they are ultimately useful for different purposes. Inference helped us understand why default risk changes, while prediction helped us decide which clients are most likely to default next month. In our inferential analysis, repayment history—especially PAY_0—was clearly important. The estimated coefficient for PAY_0 was positive and statistically significant, with a 95% confidence interval entirely above zero, showing that increased repayment delay is associated with a higher probability of default. This makes inference valuable because it provides an interpretable explanation of credit risk and identifies which factors matter most at the population level.

However, for the actual credit-risk problem, prediction is more useful. A bank ultimately needs a model that can classify future borrowers as risky or not risky, since lending decisions depend on future outcomes rather than coefficient interpretation alone. Our predictive results show why this distinction matters. The baseline logistic model produced a PR-AUC of 0.51, which is meaningfully better than random but still leaves substantial room for improvement. More importantly, its recall was only about 24%, meaning it missed most actual defaults. After class balancing, recall increased to about 64%, even though precision fell. This tradeoff makes prediction the more practically relevant framework here, because in credit risk it is often more important to detect likely defaulters than to preserve a highly conservative classifier.

Uncertainty affected the two frameworks differently. For inference, uncertainty was expressed through confidence intervals, bootstrap results, and Monte Carlo validation, which showed that the estimated default rate and the effect of repayment history were stable and precise. For prediction, uncertainty appeared through cross-validation and double bootstrapping, which showed that the model’s performance was consistent across resamples, even if only moderate in strength. In other words, inference gave the clearest explanatory insight, but prediction gave the more actionable result. For this reason, prediction is more useful for the main problem of credit-risk screening, while inference remains essential for interpreting the drivers of default and explaining the model’s conclusions.
