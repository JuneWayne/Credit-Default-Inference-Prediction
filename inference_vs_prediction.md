**Inference vs Prediction**
In this project, inference and prediction gave us related but clearly different insights about credit default. On the inferential side, the cleaned dataset contained 29,965 observations after removing 35 duplicates from the original 30,000 rows, and the default rate was about 22.1%. In the full logistic MLE model with 23 predictors, the model improved substantially over the null model, and repayment status variables emerged as some of the strongest signals. In particular, PAY_0 consistently appeared as one of the most important predictors of default risk. In the single-variable frequentist analysis, the estimated coefficient on PAY_0 was about 0.7376, and both the theory-based confidence interval [0.710, 0.766]and bootstrap interval [0.704, 0.774] excluded zero. The hypothesis test also strongly rejected the null, showing that repayment delay has a real population-level association with default. 

Prediction, however, asked a different question: not whether repayment history matters, but whether the model can accurately identify future defaulters. Here the results were more mixed. The baseline logistic model had recall of only about 24% and an AUC around 0.72, meaning it missed most actual defaults even though it performed reasonably well overall. Cross-validation showed extremely small train-test gaps and a ROC-AUC standard deviation of about ±0.013, so the model was stable and low-variance. But that same stability revealed a weakness: the model was consistently under-detecting defaults, which is a high-bias problem. The double-bootstrap results reinforced this conclusion, with PR-AUC around 0.50 ± 0.002, showing that the model’s moderate predictive ability was real and reproducible rather than the result of one favorable split. 

These results show why inference and prediction should not be treated as the same task. Inference was more useful for explanation because it told us that repayment history, especially PAY_0, has a statistically reliable and economically meaningful relationship with default risk. Prediction was more useful for decision-making because it showed that significance alone does not guarantee strong classification performance. A variable can be clearly important in an inferential sense and still be insufficient for accurate prediction when the practical goal is to catch as many defaulters as possible.

Uncertainty affected the two goals in different ways. For inference, uncertainty appeared through confidence intervals, bootstrap estimates, and hypothesis testing, all of which made the conclusion about PAY_0 more credible. For prediction, uncertainty showed up through model stability across resamples, and the cross-validation and double-bootstrap results showed that the model’s moderate performance was dependable, even if not ideal. Overall, prediction is more useful if the goal is operational risk screening, but inference is more useful for understanding why default happens and which factors drive it. In this project, inference gave the clearest substantive insight, while prediction revealed the practical limits of the current model.
